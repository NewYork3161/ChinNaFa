Chin Na Fa and the History of Chinese Fighting

Explores the origins and development of Chinese martial arts. It delves into the techniques and strategies used in combat and how they have evolved.

Lei Tai is a traditional Chinese martial arts competition that has been around for over 2000 years. This competition is known to have several similarities with Pankration. In Lei Tai matches, the fighters engaged in ground grappling with few or no rules. The Lei Tai arena was an elevated fighting platform without any railings, where fighters used to participate in bare-knuckle martial arts tournaments with potentially fatal weapons. During sanctioned matches, a referee would preside over the platform while judges watched from the sides. The loser would be determined if they surrendered, were hindered, thrown, or forced off the stage. On the other hand, the winner would remain on the stage as its "owner" unless ousted by a stronger opponent. If no more challengers were left, they would become the champion. This ancient martial art can be traced back to at least the Qin Dynasty in China and is similar to Brazilian MMA.

Chinese martial arts have had a significant impact on the development of indigenous martial arts in other countries. For instance, Chin Na techniques, rooted in Chinese martial arts, have been instrumental in shaping Aikido and Jujutsu in Japan and Hapkido in Korea. Interestingly, many of the original Chin Na techniques from Chinese martial arts share similarities with the grappling-based methods used in other forms of martial arts, such as Brazilian jiu-jitsu.

Jigoro Kano, the father of Judo and modern jiu-jitsu, acknowledges and profoundly respects Chinese martial arts for their ground fighting techniques.

Jigoro Kano, the father of Judo and modern jiu-jitsu, acknowledges and profoundly respects Chinese martial arts for their ground fighting techniques. He credits Shaolin monk Chen Yuan-Bin for bringing the Chin Na techniques that would later become Jiu-Jitsu to Japan. It is said that during the Tokugawa period (1600-1867), Hichiromen Fukuno developed a method called YawaraRyoshinto, which included the principles of Ju (the principle of climbing down or non-resistance). Around the same time, Jushin Seiguchi founded a school emphasizing Ukemi art (specifically falling). These schools systematized their techniques and created the framework for Ju-Jitsu as an independent, unarmed fighting form. Around 1600, Chen-Yuan-Bin, a warrior Shaolin monk, arrived in Japan and taught a series of procedures based on roll-outs, volts, drifts, crafty movements of throwing down on the ground, and articulated and hit techniques. He trained ronin (samurai without a master) in hand-to-hand combat techniques, such as blows in vital points and articular techniques. The instructor of the Owari samurai clan noticed Chen-Yuan-Bin's skills, and he taught them what would later be known as Ju-Jitsu. Master Jigoro Kano accredited Chen-Yuan-Bin with this. In modern times, ju-jitsu has survived through disciplines adapted by modern masters to meet the requirements of contemporary life. Judo, created by Professor Jigoro Kano, and Aikido, created by O Sensei Morihei Ueshiba, are two examples of this. Several schools continue to practice Ju-Jitsu, such as TakedaRyu, Wa-Jutsu, Ju-Tai-Jutsu (within Ninjutsu Bujinkan), Goshinjutsu (Ju-jitsu for defense), and Kendo (the way of the sword).

First published in 1936, this work represents primary source material of ancient combat techniques designed in a time of occupation and war, when the threat of lethal hand-to-hand combat was an ever-present reality for soldiers, those involved in law enforcement, and very often for the ordinary citizen. This is the seminal work in the field, written by the form’s founders, Liu Jinsheng and Zhao Jiang, as a training manual for the Police Academy of Zheijiang province. The intent of this translation is to provide authentic historical documentation for martial arts techniques that have been modified for use today in both competition and self-defense. Submission grappling is a technique in which fighters use locks, chokes, and breaking techniques to defeat their challengers in no-holds-barred matches. Chi Na Fa remains the most comprehensive explanation available of these Chinese grappling techniques, from which derive many current techniques. Renowned author and Brazillian jiu jitsu champion Tim Cartmell presents the book in a clear, compelling new translation.
